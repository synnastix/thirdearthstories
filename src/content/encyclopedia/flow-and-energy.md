*Musings on the nature of the worlds energies, compiled from notes in the Imperial Archives, including banned works.*

## The Flow Itself

The energy has no single name. The Empire calls it flow. The Visareen call it mana. Those without a framework call it magic. The Wildkin have no word for it at all - naming it would imply separation from it, and they recognize no such separation.

It moves through everything. Stone and bird and sky and soil, the living and the dead, the practitioner and the world being practiced upon. It streams and surfaces, dives underground and returns. It does not favor any single vessel or framework. It does not distinguish between the wizard drawing it through a mirror-bowl, the Temple healer asking to receive it, and the wind moving across an open plain.

The disagreement between institutions is not about the energy itself. It is about what relationship a person is permitted to have with it - extractive, devotional, integrative - and what that relationship says about the institution claiming it. The energy remains indifferent to all of them.

Those who have seen it clearly tend to stop arguing about frameworks.

## Legion Perspective

The Legion approaches flow, which it sometimes refers to generically as the world’s energy system, as a tool to be understood, assigned, and controlled. Every practitioner is evaluated before training and assigned to the discipline most aligned with their essence: scrying, conjuration, illusion, destruction, among others. 

The underlying mechanic is consistent across all disciplines: energy is drawn in from the world around the practitioner, stored briefly in the body, then focused and released toward a purpose.

The toll is real and cumulative. Scrying clouds the eyes and eventually milks them over. Combat mages burn fast and die young. Minds trained in silent speech develop waking echoes they can never fully quiet. Those who attempt to step between spaces sometimes emerge wrong in ways that are hard to name. When possible, the Legion mitigates this through object channeling - instruments that absorb the cost of channeling so the practitioner's body doesn't have to bear the full weight.

The Legion doesn't deny the danger. It manages it, allocates it, and sends the bill to whoever is least valuable. Any discipline is theoretically available to any practitioner willing to pay the cost. But the Legion discourages wandering outside assigned disciplines - not out of ignorance, but pragmatism. The cost of learning what you weren't built for tends to be paid in years.

## Temple Perspective

The Temple practitioners do not draw energy - they receives it. Where the Legion sees a resource to be extracted, the Temple's practitioners see themselves as vessels. Mana flows into them not because they reached for it, but because they asked, and were answered.

The discipline is devotional rather than technical. A Temple healer does not study the mechanics of restoration; they study the nature of the gods who favor it. The results are real - wounds close, fevers break, the dying sometimes don't - but the Temple attributes the mechanism entirely to divine will. That the gods might be channeling the same energy the Legion deploys through mirror-bowls and combat mages is not a question the Temple entertains seriously. The energy is a gift. Asking where it comes from is ingratitude.

## Wildkin Perspective

Where the Empire categorizes and contains, the Wildkin relationship to the world's energy is integrative rather than extractive. It flows through them as it flows through the land – the Visareen singing to stars even as their forests burn, the Somari moving through desert terrain with an intimacy no Imperial map can replicate. The Wildkin don't draw energy in and release it; they exist within it. 

## Unresolved Phenomena

Not everything mana does fits neatly into any framework. There are examples of mana interacting with the world without a practitioner directing it. Uncontrolled, ambient, sometimes dangerous.

The Legion files these under unsolved problems. The Temple attributes them to gods and restless spirits. The Wildkin, who live closest to the flow, tend to treat them as simply another natural phenomenon rather than miracle. None of the these formal frameworks fully accounts for them, and none of the institutions is comfortable admitting that.
