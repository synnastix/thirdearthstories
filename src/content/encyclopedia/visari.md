## Culture

The Visari are a people organized around memory and reverence -  for the land, for the dead, for the weight of what came before. Their settlements grow into the redwood canopy rather than displacing it, platforms strung between ancient limbs, whole generations living in the heights without ever touching soil. This is not merely architecture; it is theology. The trees are not resources, they are relationships. Their elders speak in metaphor because metaphor is more precise. Their legends travel in the bodies of their singers, not in books, which means their enemies can burn their settlements but cannot burn their history. It lives in the people who survived.

## Politics

Leadership falls to women, the ones who understand what needs remembering, who carry the weight of the people between conflicts.

Internally, The Visari are a matriarchal society where the elder females are charged with holding the memory of the people and passing wisdom to future generations. The many tribes each have their own structure and cooperate with each other to preserve their forest as a living sanctuary.

Externally, the loose tribes present themselves as a Dominion with a central leadership structure. This is a formality only that caters to the other factions, notably the Empire, as female leadership is not well respected by most groups. 


## Economy

The Visari economy is inseparable from the forest itself. They work with the land rather than shaping it, which places them in direct and irreconcilable conflict with the Khuldinai appetite for redwood. The trees are dense, sacred, and vast enough to fuel machines and ambition, which means the Visari are sitting on top of exactly what the industrial world wants most. 

A lesser-accepted quality of Visari economics is their involvement in the illicit trade markets through cartels. While this practice is denounced officially, in truth the Visari do not see it as something forbidden. Rather, the Visari recognize the interconnectedness of all things and view the cartel operations as a natural mechanism of connection.
